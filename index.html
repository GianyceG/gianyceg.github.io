Introduction

The use of time series analysis gained widespread recognition in the 1970s with the popularization of ARIMA models by the Box-Jenkins method. However, the concept of analyzing sequential data over time has been around for much longer. As the world increasingly turns toward data-driven solutions for future industry outcomes, time series analysis has become crucial across various fields, including public health initiatives in response to COVID-19 and economic studies like forecasting income distribution gaps.

In this capstone project, I will explore two different datasets: the COVID-19 data from Johns Hopkins CSSEGISandData and Our World in Data (OWID), and socioeconomic data from the U.S. Census. Using these datasets, I will apply ARIMA/SARIMA models for capturing trends and seasonality in univariate time series and Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS) for incorporating uncertainty into the forecasts.

Methods

This project explores time series forecasting using two distinct datasets: one focused on COVID-19 data and the other on U.S. Census data. I will begin by explaining the methods I plan to apply to the COVID-19 dataset.

For the COVID-19 dataset, I will use time series forecasting with an emphasis on ARIMA/SARIMA and Bayesian Structural Time Series (BSTS) models. ARIMA models are univariate (single-variable) time series models without trends or seasonal components, while SARIMA can capture multivariate series with trends and seasonal patterns. ARIMA is particularly effective for forecasting in this context because it applies mathematical techniques to smooth non-stationary time series data, making it easier to predict future values based on historical trends.
